## What actually happens when you push AI music too hard

Suno exports tracks at around -10 to -12 LUFS. That's already on the hot side. When you try to make it louder on top of that, you push the peaks above 0 dBFS — the absolute ceiling of digital audio.

What happens at the ceiling: the waveform gets cut flat. That flat cut is called clipping. It sounds like a harsh buzz on loud moments. A brittleness. A distortion that doesn't go away no matter what you do next, because the information that was supposed to be there is simply gone.

And then Spotify or YouTube receives your track and normalizes it to their target — -14 LUFS for Spotify. They turn it down. The clipping stays. Now you have a quiet clipped track instead of a loud clipped track. The worst of both worlds.

## What loudness actually means on streaming

Streaming platforms don't care how loud your file is. They normalize everything to the same target. A track at -10 LUFS and a track at -14 LUFS will play at roughly the same perceived volume on Spotify — because Spotify turns the loud one down.

What you can control is how clean and well-balanced the track is at that normalized level. That's what mastering actually does. Not make it louder. Make it sound good at the volume the platform is going to play it at.

When I understood this, the whole thing made more sense.

## The practical answer to making your track sound bigger

Since this is what most people are actually looking for — here is what I found actually works:

**Target -14 LUFS, not 0 dBFS.** The number you want to hit is the platform target, not the loudness ceiling. -14 LUFS on Spotify sounds just as loud as anything else on Spotify — because everything else is also at -14 LUFS.

**Fix clipping before normalizing.** If your track clips, fix the peaks first with a true peak limiter, then normalize loudness. The other order doesn't work.

**Don't over-compress.** Heavy compression makes a track dense but not loud. It also removes the dynamic variation that makes music feel alive. The track sounds flat and tiring instead of powerful.

**Check on different speakers.** What sounds loud on studio monitors might sound thin on phone speakers. What sounds balanced on headphones might have too much bass on laptop speakers. Listen on at least two different devices before deciding you're done.

**Use the Analyze tab first.** Before touching any settings, look at what you actually have. LUFS, True Peak, Dynamic Range — these numbers tell you what the problem is and how to fix it without guessing.
