*By Serj · July 2026 · 6 min read*

I want to tell you about something YouTube did that I still think was wrong. Not illegal — I have no idea about that. Just wrong.

## What happened

I had a Suno track that started doing well on YouTube. A few hundred thousand views. Then more. People were watching it, sharing it, leaving comments. The algorithm was picking it up.

Monetization was active. YouTube was running ads. Their ads, in front of my video, while the song climbed.

Then at some point YouTube decided my content was "template-based" — that I was producing AI music at scale using a pattern. They removed monetization. The ads stopped too.

I don't have a strong argument against that decision by itself. Maybe they're right that AI music channels shouldn't be monetized the same way. Their platform, their rules. I understood that from the start.

What I don't accept is what happened to the period before that.

When monetization was active and ads were running — YouTube collected that ad revenue. Then they removed my monetization and I never saw any of it. The money from the time when the song was actually popular, when their ads were actually showing — none of that came to me.

They ran the ads. They collected the money. Then they closed the door and kept everything.

That's the part I find hard to swallow. If my content wasn't eligible for monetization, tell me that before you run ads on it — not after you've already collected the revenue from the period when it actually mattered.

I'm not going to pretend this destroyed me financially. The amounts involved were not life-changing. But the principle bothers me. I worked on those tracks. People watched them. YouTube profited. I got nothing.

## Then came the copying

While all of this was happening, other people were copying the tracks.

Someone would take a song, change three words in the lyrics, reupload it as their own. I'd find my tracks — or recognizable versions of them — in TikTok clips, in Reels, in YouTube Shorts. Sometimes credited to strangers with names I didn't recognize. Sometimes just floating there with no attribution at all.

The good ones got copied. The ones that didn't do well were left alone. I noticed this pattern and decided to interpret it as a quality filter: if someone found the song worth stealing, it was probably good enough to steal.

That reframe helped. Not completely. But enough.

## Who actually owns Suno output

Here is the part that makes the copyright question complicated.

Suno's terms of service, as of 2026, give creators rights to use their output, including for commercial purposes on paid plans. But they retain certain rights to the generated content, and the legal status of AI-generated music is still genuinely unsettled in most jurisdictions. TOS can change — check Suno's current terms before making decisions based on them.

The practical reality: if someone copies your Suno track and you want to pursue it legally, you're entering complicated territory. You didn't write the underlying composition in the traditional sense. An AI model generated it in response to your prompt. Courts in different countries are still figuring out what that means for ownership.

I'm not a lawyer. I'm not giving legal advice. I'm telling you what I concluded for myself after thinking about it.

My conclusion: the legal protection for AI music output, as it currently stands, is weak enough that pursuing infringement cases would cost more in time and money than I would likely recover. Especially for the kind of copying that was happening to me — small channels, modified lyrics, no real commercial scale.

## What I actually did

Nothing. I decided not to fight it.

Not because I was fine with it. Because I did the math and decided my time was better spent making new tracks than chasing down people who stole old ones.

The songs that got copied kept existing. Some of them kept circulating — even the stolen versions, in their modified form, were reaching people who might not have found the original. I'm not going to pretend that doesn't sting a little. But it's the reality.

The YouTube situation I'm still annoyed about. But there's no mechanism for disputing ad revenue that was collected during a period that later got reclassified. That money is gone.

## What this actually means for you

If you're making AI music and thinking about rights and platforms, here is what I think is worth knowing:

**Monetization on YouTube is fragile for AI music.** The policies are still evolving. If a channel gets flagged as template-based or mass-produced, monetization can be removed retroactively in effect — as I experienced. Don't build a business plan that depends on YouTube ad revenue from AI music being stable.

**Copying will happen if your work is good.** There is currently no reliable mechanism to stop it at scale. You can file individual copyright claims, and some will be resolved in your favor. But it's a slow process and the people doing the copying usually have nothing worth recovering.

**The platform always wins in the short term.** YouTube, TikTok, Spotify — they set the rules and they change them. The only real leverage you have is being able to move your audience somewhere else, and that takes time to build.

**None of this means you shouldn't make the music.** I still make tracks. I still upload them. The situation isn't fair, but most situations involving large platforms and individual creators aren't fair. You make something, you put it out, you see what happens.

## The part I keep coming back to

What bothers me most about the YouTube situation isn't the money. It's the asymmetry.

They built a system that runs ads on content. They profited from my content while it was popular. Then they changed the classification and kept what they earned. I had no recourse and no warning.

That's not a legal complaint — I don't know if any law was broken. It's a moral complaint. If you're going to run ads on someone's content and collect money from those ads, you should pay them for that content. Running the ads and keeping the money feels like taking something that wasn't yours to take.

I'm aware this is just one person's opinion about a situation that affects millions of creators. I'm also aware that platform companies are not structured to care about one person's opinion.

But I think it's worth saying clearly: what they did wasn't right, even if it was within the rules they wrote for themselves.
